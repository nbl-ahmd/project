\abstract{\OnePageChapter
Handwritten medical prescriptions remain common in many healthcare settings and are difficult to digitize because they contain mixed printed and handwritten content, inconsistent writing styles, abbreviations, dosage patterns, and noisy mobile-phone images. This thesis presents an end-to-end framework for medical prescription recognition that converts a full prescription image into structured medication information. The proposed system performs page preprocessing, handwritten-region detection, line and word cropping, TrOCR-based medicine-name recognition, and post-OCR validation using dosage-frequency rules and drug lexicon matching. The implementation includes Streamlit and notebook-based annotation tools, a word-level dataset builder, and a YOLO layout-training workflow for improving handwritten-region crops. The OCR module was evaluated on the Doctor's Handwritten Prescription BD dataset, where a fine-tuned \texttt{microsoft/trocr-base-handwritten} model achieved 96.28\% exact-match accuracy, 2.87\% character error rate, and 3.92\% word error rate on 780 testing samples. The final system demonstrates a practical pathway from full prescription images to reviewed word-level training data and structured medication predictions, while separating quantitative OCR results from the still-expanding page-level dataset.
}

\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chap:introduction}

Handwritten prescriptions continue to be used in several healthcare environments because they are fast to write, require little infrastructure, and fit established clinical habits. However, handwritten prescriptions are also a major source of ambiguity in pharmacy workflows. Medication names may be visually similar, dosage instructions may be abbreviated, and scanned or photographed prescriptions may contain blur, shadows, skew, and background noise. These conditions make manual interpretation error-prone and make direct whole-page optical character recognition unreliable.

The problem becomes more difficult because prescriptions are not homogeneous text documents. A single page may contain a printed clinic header, patient details, date fields, handwritten medicines, dosage instructions, signatures, and footer notes. General-purpose OCR systems often process the whole image uniformly, although the clinically important information is concentrated in specific handwritten zones. A region-aware system is therefore required so that the handwritten prescription area can be isolated before recognition.

Recent work on medical handwriting recognition has shown that domain-specific datasets and recognition models can improve accuracy. Bhuiyan et al. reported the need for systems that can recognise medical terms in busy healthcare settings and introduced a medical-term recognition dataset with data augmentation for handwritten words \cite{bhuiyan2022online}. Biomedical language models such as BioBERT improve downstream biomedical text mining tasks \cite{lee2020biobert}, while transformer-based OCR models such as TrOCR provide a strong visual-to-text architecture for handwritten word recognition \cite{li2023trocr}. Object detection models such as YOLO provide a practical way to locate regions of interest in document images \cite{jocher2023yolo}. This thesis combines these ideas into a modular prescription recognition workflow.

\section{Motivation}

The motivation for this work is to reduce the gap between handwritten clinical practice and digital healthcare workflows. A pharmacist or healthcare worker should be able to provide a full prescription image as input and obtain structured medicine information as output. The system should not depend only on the OCR model, because recognition accuracy depends strongly on whether the correct handwritten region and word crop are supplied to the OCR module. The implementation therefore treats cropping, annotation, OCR, and validation as connected stages.

\section{Problem Definition}
\label{sec:problem-definition}

The problem addressed in this thesis is the automatic extraction of medication information from full handwritten prescription images. The input to the system is a prescription page image captured by a scanner or mobile camera, and the expected output is a structured record containing detected medicine names, dosage expressions, frequency expressions, confidence or validation status, and intermediate crop references for review. The solution must segment handwritten prescription regions from printed content, crop relevant line and word images, recognise medicine names, and validate the recognised text against medical-domain constraints.

\section{Objectives}

The first objective of this work is to design an end-to-end modular pipeline for prescription image processing. The second objective is to implement annotation tools that convert raw full prescription images into reviewed word-level OCR training samples similar to the public cropped-word dataset used during model development. The third objective is to fine-tune and evaluate a TrOCR model for medicine-name recognition. The fourth objective is to introduce a learned region-cropping workflow using YOLO so that inaccurate heuristic crops can be replaced by a detector trained on corrected prescription-region boxes. The final objective is to integrate OCR output with rule-based dosage and frequency extraction and lexicon-based medicine validation.

\chapter{Literature Survey}
\label{chap:literature}

Handwritten prescription recognition has been studied from several perspectives, including handwriting recognition, document layout analysis, medical terminology correction, and structured information retrieval. This chapter reviews the works that motivated the proposed system.

\section{Handwritten Medical Word Recognition}

Bhuiyan et al. proposed an online cursive handwritten medical words recognition system for Bangladesh \cite{bhuiyan2022online}. Their study emphasized that handwritten prescriptions are still common in developing countries and that illegibility may lead to incorrect medication and dosage decisions. The authors developed a handwritten medical term corpus and used sequence-based recognition methods with augmentation. Their reported results show that domain-specific handwriting data is essential for medical prescription recognition. This work motivates the use of medical prescription word datasets rather than relying only on generic OCR datasets.

Tabassum et al. also investigated handwritten medical-term recognition and emphasized the benefit of Rotate-Shift-Stretch augmentation for improving recognition robustness \cite{tabassum2022online}. Their work is important because it shows that handwriting variation must be explicitly addressed during training. Although their approach is based on recurrent models and online handwriting information, the same principle applies to image-based OCR: a model must be exposed to sufficient variation in medicine-name writing styles.

\section{Prescription Recognition in Multilingual and Cursive Settings}

Pavithiran et al. studied handwritten prescription recognition in multilingual settings using deep learning \cite{pavithiran2024multilingual}. Their approach combined image preprocessing, segmentation, fuzzy search, and sequence recognition. This is relevant to the present thesis because prescriptions in Indian and South Asian contexts may contain English medicine names, local trade names, abbreviations, and regional-language notes. The work suggests that OCR alone is insufficient and that post-processing using domain knowledge is necessary.

Shaw et al. proposed a neural-network-based prescription recognition and information retrieval method \cite{utkarshaw2021}. Their system used character recognition and retrieval methods to interpret medical handwriting. While their reported performance indicates the usefulness of neural networks for prescription text, character-level segmentation can be fragile for cursive writing. This motivates the word-level OCR approach used in this thesis, where the model directly predicts a medicine name from a cropped word image.

\section{Transformer-based OCR}

TrOCR uses a transformer-based encoder-decoder architecture for optical character recognition \cite{li2023trocr}. The model combines visual feature extraction with sequence generation, allowing the OCR system to model long-range dependencies in handwritten text. In this thesis, \texttt{microsoft/trocr-base-handwritten} is fine-tuned for medicine-name recognition because the previously available training dataset consists of cropped handwritten medicine-name words. The use of TrOCR also allows presentation-time inference on individual word crops produced from full prescription images.

\section{Document Region Detection}

Medical prescriptions are visually rich documents and require layout analysis before OCR. Object detection and segmentation methods such as Mask R-CNN \cite{he2017mask} and YOLO \cite{jocher2023yolo} can be used to locate regions of interest. A YOLO-based detector is particularly suitable for this project because it is lightweight, fast, and easy to train from bounding-box annotations. The implementation therefore includes a YOLO handwritten-region training workflow so that inaccurate heuristic crops can be replaced by a learned region detector.

SAM can also be used to accelerate mask or box annotation, but it does not remove the need for corrected labelled examples. In this thesis, SAM is treated as a possible annotation assistant, while YOLO is used as the practical learned cropper for repeated inference.

\section{Biomedical Text Validation}

Biomedical text validation is important because OCR output may contain spelling errors or visually similar medicine names. BioBERT was introduced as a biomedical language representation model trained on biomedical corpora and shown to improve biomedical text mining tasks \cite{lee2020biobert}. Although a full BioBERT named-entity recogniser was not trained in the current implementation, the thesis implements the validation layer using drug lexicon matching and regular expressions for dosage and frequency extraction. This modular design allows a BioBERT-based NER model to be integrated later without changing the earlier image-processing stages.

\section{Pipeline Formulation}

Let the input prescription image be represented as $I$. The preprocessing stage transforms $I$ into a cleaned page image $P$ by resizing, deskewing, denoising, and contrast enhancement. The page transformation is represented in Equation \ref{eq:preprocessing}.

\begin{equation}
P = f_{pre}(I)
\label{eq:preprocessing}
\end{equation}
\listequation{Preprocessing transformation from raw prescription image to cleaned page image}

The layout stage detects a set of handwritten regions $R = \{r_1, r_2, \ldots, r_n\}$ from the cleaned page image. In the final implementation this detector can be a trained YOLO model, manually exported YOLO labels, or a heuristic fallback. The learned detector is preferred because it estimates the region from corrected training boxes rather than from hand-crafted image thresholds. The detection stage is represented in Equation \ref{eq:region-detection}.

\begin{equation}
R = f_{layout}(P)
\label{eq:region-detection}
\end{equation}
\listequation{Detection of handwritten prescription regions from the preprocessed page}

Each region is then segmented into line crops and word crops. If $L$ denotes the set of line crops and $W$ denotes the set of word crops, the segmentation stage is represented in Equation \ref{eq:segmentation}. The current OCR model is word-level, so the final recognition unit is a word crop.

\begin{equation}
W = f_{word}\bigl(f_{line}(R)\bigr)
\label{eq:segmentation}
\end{equation}
\listequation{Line and word crop generation from detected handwritten regions}

For each word crop $w_i$, the OCR model predicts a text string $\hat{y_i}$. The TrOCR model is a sequence generation model, and the prediction is represented in Equation \ref{eq:ocr}.

\begin{equation}
\hat{y_i} = f_{ocr}(w_i)
\label{eq:ocr}
\end{equation}
\listequation{OCR prediction for a cropped handwritten word image}

The validation stage compares the OCR output with a medicine lexicon and extracts dosage and frequency expressions using rules. If $d(\hat{y_i}, m_j)$ is the edit distance between the OCR string and a candidate medicine name, the lexicon match can be expressed as the medicine name that maximizes the normalized similarity score in Equation \ref{eq:lexicon}.

\begin{equation}
m^* = \arg\max_{m_j \in M}\left(1 - \frac{d(\hat{y_i}, m_j)}{\max(|\hat{y_i}|, |m_j|)}\right)
\label{eq:lexicon}
\end{equation}
\listequation{Lexicon-based medicine-name correction using normalized edit similarity}

\section{Evaluation Metrics}

The OCR module is evaluated using exact-match accuracy, character error rate, and word error rate. Exact-match accuracy measures the fraction of predictions that exactly match the reference medicine name, as shown in Equation \ref{eq:accuracy}.

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Accuracy} = \frac{N_{correct}}{N_{total}} \times 100
\label{eq:accuracy}
\end{equation}
\listequation{Exact-match accuracy for medicine-name recognition}

Character error rate measures character-level edit distance normalized by the number of reference characters, as shown in Equation \ref{eq:cer}. Word error rate is computed similarly at the word-token level, as shown in Equation \ref{eq:wer}. These metrics are standard for OCR evaluation because they report both strict correctness and partial transcription errors.

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CER} = \frac{S_c + D_c + I_c}{N_c}
\label{eq:cer}
\end{equation}
\listequation{Character error rate for OCR output}

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{WER} = \frac{S_w + D_w + I_w}{N_w}
\label{eq:wer}
\end{equation}
\listequation{Word error rate for OCR output}

In Equations \ref{eq:cer} and \ref{eq:wer}, $S$, $D$, and $I$ denote substitutions, deletions, and insertions, while $N$ denotes the number of reference characters or words.

\section{Annotation Workflow}

The annotation workflow was implemented because full prescription images cannot be used directly by the word-level OCR model. The raw image must first be transformed into accurate word crops. The implemented workflow starts with page preprocessing and region annotation. A manual notebook cropper and a CVAT-compatible export path are provided so that handwritten regions can be corrected. The corrected region boxes can then be used to train the YOLO detector described in Section \ref{sec:yolo-layout}.

After region cropping, the system performs line segmentation and word segmentation. Each word crop is stored with a manifest row containing its source page, source line, crop path, and page-level coordinates. The word annotation tool allows the annotator to fill the visible word text, medicine name, medicine flag, confidence level, review status, and notes. Reviewed medicine-name rows can then be exported to a dataset structure matching the Doctor's Handwritten Prescription BD dataset. This design allows the custom full-page data to become usable for the same TrOCR training code.

\section{YOLO-based Region Cropper}
\label{sec:yolo-layout}

The initial cropper used a heuristic image-processing method that estimated the handwritten region from thresholded connected components. This method was useful for demonstration but was not sufficiently reliable for final evaluation. Therefore, the implementation now includes a YOLO layout-training workflow. Corrected handwritten-region boxes are converted into a YOLO dataset using \texttt{prepare\_yolo\_layout\_dataset.py}, and the detector is trained using \texttt{train\_yolo\_layout.py}. During inference, \texttt{run\_end\_to\_end.py} can load the trained \texttt{best.pt} model and crop the handwritten region using detector predictions. This provides a learned cropper that improves as more corrected prescription boxes are annotated.

\section{OCR Training}

The OCR model is based on \texttt{microsoft/trocr-base-handwritten}. The model was fine-tuned on the medicine-name column of the cropped-word dataset. The training notebook freezes the encoder for faster training on limited hardware and fine-tunes the decoder for medicine-name generation. The evaluation uses the testing split and computes exact-match accuracy, CER, and WER.

\section{Post-OCR Validation}

The post-OCR validation layer uses two mechanisms. The first mechanism extracts dosage and frequency patterns using regular expressions. This identifies expressions such as ``500 mg'', ``650 mg'', ``1-0-1'', and ``BD''. The second mechanism compares OCR output with a drug lexicon using fuzzy similarity. This can correct minor OCR mistakes when the predicted text is close to a known medicine name. For example, a noisy prediction such as ``Acita'' can be matched to ``Aceta'' if its similarity score crosses the configured threshold. This validation stage is not a replacement for clinical review, but it improves interpretability and highlights uncertain predictions.

\section{Discussion}

The experimental results show that medicine-name OCR is strong when the crop is correct. This is an important observation because it separates the OCR problem from the full-page segmentation problem. In the early implementation, inaccurate region crops reduced the usefulness of the final pipeline even though the OCR model itself performed well on clean word crops. This is why the final implementation includes both a word-level annotation workflow and a YOLO layout-training workflow.

The full prescription pipeline currently demonstrates the integration of preprocessing, region detection, line segmentation, word segmentation, OCR inference, and validation. However, the page-level quantitative evaluation is not yet as mature as the word-level OCR evaluation because a sufficiently large reviewed full-page dataset must still be completed. The implemented annotation tools directly address this limitation by allowing raw prescriptions to be converted into reviewed word crops and corrected region boxes.

\section{Limitations}

The current system has three main limitations. First, the OCR model was trained on cropped medicine names, so it requires accurate word crops during final inference. Second, dosage and frequency extraction is rule-based and may miss uncommon clinical abbreviations. Third, the learned YOLO cropper depends on the number and quality of corrected handwritten-region boxes. These limitations do not invalidate the modular architecture, but they define the next stages required before deployment in a clinical workflow.

\chapter{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{chap:conclusion}

This thesis presented a region-aware medical prescription recognition framework for efficient healthcare workflows. The system accepts full prescription images, preprocesses the page, detects handwritten regions, segments line and word crops, recognises medicine names using TrOCR, and validates OCR output using dosage-frequency rules and drug lexicon matching. The implemented annotation tools allow raw prescription images to be transformed into reviewed word-level OCR datasets, while the YOLO training workflow addresses the inaccurate crop problem observed in the heuristic cropper.

The fine-tuned TrOCR model achieved 96.28\% exact-match accuracy, 2.87\% CER, and 3.92\% WER on the cropped-word testing split. These results confirm that transformer-based OCR is effective for medicine-name recognition when supplied with clean word crops. The final implementation extends the work beyond OCR by introducing the infrastructure needed for full-prescription processing and future page-level evaluation.

Future work should focus on completing a larger reviewed full-page prescription dataset, quantitatively evaluating the trained YOLO region detector, and training the OCR model on custom word crops extracted from local prescriptions. The validation layer can be improved by integrating a BioBERT-based NER model and a larger Indian drug-name lexicon. Additional privacy-preserving preprocessing should also be added so that patient identifiers are masked before annotation or training.
